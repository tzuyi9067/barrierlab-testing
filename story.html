Our Story

Thirty years of eczema. One system that finally worked.

Barrier Lab was built by John — a guy who spent three decades as an eczema patient before he was a founder. Every cream. Every elimination diet. Every prescription. This is what he landed on, and why he's sharing it.

The origin

It started when I was four years old, and didn't let up until I was thirty-four.

I've had eczema since I was a kid. Insides of my elbows, backs of my knees, patches on my neck that showed up in every school photo. My mom kept a plastic bin under the bathroom sink full of every cream a pediatrician had ever recommended. None of it ever really worked — they'd take a flare down for a week, then it'd come back worse.

By my teens I was on steroid cycles. By my twenties I'd done three elimination diets, a "gut cleanse" I still feel bad about spending money on, and two rounds of Dupixent injections that helped but cost more than my rent. I'd given up on the idea that I was ever going to not itch.

In my early thirties something changed. I started reading the atopic dermatitis research myself — not the blog posts about it, the actual papers. And what I found was that the people who got durable relief weren't doing one thing. They were doing two things at once: rebuilding the skin barrier from the outside, and quieting the gut-immune axis from the inside. Most products only do one or the other. Nobody was selling the full routine.

So I built it. First for myself. Then, when it kept working, for anyone else who was tired of getting only half an answer.

What finally worked

Outside

A ceramide + niacinamide balm morning and night. No fragrance, no steroid. Became Recovery Balm.

Inside

A daily probiotic + lactoferrin + zinc + D3 stack. Took six weeks to kick in; the difference was unmistakable by week eight. Became Inner Barrier Complex (and, for kids, Inner Powder).

The long version

Thirty years, one stubborn patient, one system that stuck.

Age 4

First flare

Eczema shows up for the first time. Pediatrician prescribes hydrocortisone. Mom starts the cream collection under the bathroom sink.

Age 12

First dermatologist

Stronger topical steroids. Lotion rotation. A list of foods to avoid. I stop wearing shorts to PE.

Age 19

The itch year

Worst flare of my life, mid-college. Allergist tells me it's "chronic" and I should get used to it. I don't get used to it.

Age 26

Elimination diets + Dupixent

Thirteen weeks of cutting out dairy, gluten, and eggs. Then a year on biologics. Relief, but expensive and partial — the flares shrank, they didn't leave.

Age 31

Reading the research myself

I start pulling atopic dermatitis papers on the gut-skin axis. Lactoferrin trials. Probiotic meta-analyses. The pattern no one had ever told me about.

Age 33

The personal prototype

I build a two-part routine from the literature: a ceramide-heavy balm and a daily oral stack. Six weeks in, my skin is the calmest it's been since I was three.

2026 — now

What we believe

Three things we won't compromise on.

Both, not either.

Eczema is a two-front problem. We refuse to sell only the outside or only the inside, even though narrower lines are easier to market.

Clinical dosing.

If an ingredient is in the bottle, it's at a dose the research actually supports. No "also contains" trace amounts. No label-only formulas.

Honest claims.

Eczema is chronic. We won't promise cures. What we promise: better days, fewer flares, and a system you can stay on without thinking about it.
